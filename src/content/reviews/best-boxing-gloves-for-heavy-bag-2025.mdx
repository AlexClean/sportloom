      <h2 className="text-2xl sm:text-3xl font-extrabold text-slate-900 text-center">Bag Gloves vs Sparring Gloves — Why Separate Pairs Matter</h2>
      <div className="mx-auto mt-4 max-w-5xl rounded-2xl border border-blue-200/60 bg-white p-5 shadow-sm ring-1 ring-blue-900/5">
        <p className="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed"> Heavy bags don’t hit back — they reflect your force straight into your hands. That’s why bag gloves typically use denser foams and tougher outer materials to survive repetitive impact. Sparring gloves, by contrast, are softer to protect your partner and distribute force more generously. Can you use one pair for both? You can, but it’s a compromise. Bag work rapidly compresses softer sparring foams, and dense bag foams can be too harsh on a training partner. Most coaches recommend two pairs: one for the bag, one for sparring. Your knuckles — and training partners — will thank you.</p>

      <h2 className="text-2xl sm:text-3xl font-extrabold text-slate-900 text-center">Key Buying Factors for Heavy Bag Gloves</h2>

        <h3 className="font-semibold text-xl">Padding Density &amp; Distribution</h3>
        <p className="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Look for multi-layer or gel-reinforced systems that keep knuckles comfortable without feeling like pillows. Good bag gloves give assertive feedback — you feel the shot — but don’t punish the joints.</p>
        <h3 className="font-semibold text-xl mt-4">Wrist Support</h3>
        <p className="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Dual-strap systems (like Hayabusa T3) or robust hook-and-loop wraps help maintain a straight wrist on heavy shots. If you’ve ever felt wrist collapse on a hook, you know how essential this is.</p>
        <h3 className="font-semibold text-xl mt-4">Materials &amp; Durability</h3>
        <p className="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Full-grain leather generally lasts longer and ages better; quality synthetics can still be great value. Inspect stitching around the thumb and palm — these are common failure points with bag volume.</p>
        <h3 className="font-semibold text-xl mt-4">Fit &amp; Hand Compartment</h3>
        <p className="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Slim/profiles give crisp feel and speed; roomier fits suit larger hands and thicker wraps. Pre-curved interiors help newer boxers form a proper fist and minimize hand fatigue.</p>
        <h3 className="font-semibold text-xl mt-4">Weight (12–16 oz)</h3>
        <p className="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">12–14 oz suit most bag work; 16 oz are popular for conditioning or bigger athletes. Lighter gloves feel faster but protect less; heavier gloves build endurance and cushion impact.</p>

      <h2 className="text-2xl sm:text-3xl font-extrabold text-slate-900 text-center">Common Mistakes When Choosing Bag Gloves</h2>
      <div className="mx-auto mt-4 max-w-5xl rounded-2xl border border-blue-200/60 bg-white p-5 shadow-sm ring-1 ring-blue-900/5">
        <ul className="list-disc pl-6 text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">
          <li><strong>Going too light.</strong> 10 oz feels quick but can be unforgiving for knuckles and wrists on the bag.</li>
          <li><strong>Using sparring pillows for bag work.</strong> They’ll compress fast and teach vague mechanics.</li>
          <li><strong>Ignoring wrist support.</strong> If your wrist collapses on hooks, you’ll lose power and risk injury.</li>
          <li><strong>Buying the wrong fit.</strong> Slim gloves with thick wraps can cramp the hand and cause hot spots; too roomy and the fist shifts on impact.</li>
          <li><strong>Skipping hand wraps.</strong> Wraps add structure and absorb sweat; they’re not optional for bag rounds.</li>
        </ul>
